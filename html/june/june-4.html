June Variable

It seems as though the old man from the bar may be more trustworthy than my initial assessment. After a surprisingly resftful night's sleep, we headed to meet him.

He lives in a Winnebago a hundred yards back from the Bar. It's been out here so long it fades into the landscape.

Like a chameleon, its once-white paint is now the same light orange as the sand -- even the rust holes in the wheel wells look like the shadows in the dunes.

I am reminded of what the Bartender said, about how people come here to hide, just like desert fauna camouflage themselves in plain sight.

Fortunately, the old man is significantly more hospitable than his surroundings and he warmly welcomed us into his humble lodgings. He introduced himself as "[our] Intrepid Leopold." We sat at his Formica table and sipped watery powdered lemonade from plastic cups.

After what I deemed to be an appropriately polite amount of time, I asked. "So you mentioned that you too are looking for someone. Can you tell us a little more about that?"

Leopold didn't say anything for a moment, just dragged the cup across the table, the rim leaving a wet glistened wave.

For another beat, we all stood transfixed at the cup's comet-tail arching across the table.

"Oh, I'm afraid I can't tell you that," he said, though not unkindly. "But I can show you something that might pique your interest."

The old man led us to the edge of the dune that his camper rested on and pointed towards the horizon.

"See how they sparkle?" Leopold said, and I noticed the tiny flashing bursts of light in the distance, which I had originally written off as a symptom of heat stroke. .

"Just like the stars." His whole face softened to expose a raw element of affection and adoration, which almost touched me.

"It's far too hot to try and make it there during the daylight hours. You can only make the trek at night. I'll swing by your folks' campground this evening and we'll head out together."
